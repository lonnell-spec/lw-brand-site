The Big Idea

Spontaneity Is Not a Strategy.

Hoping you get the shot is not a workflow. Showing up and figuring it out is not a plan — it's a prayer. And while prayer is good, God also honors preparation.

The stories that move people — new families through the door, the city seeing what God is doing in this room — don't happen by accident. They are planned. Assigned. Shot with intention. Edited with purpose.

The gap between what your team produces and what the church deserves is a planning problem. Not a talent problem.
You don't need more volunteers. You need a plan your volunteers can follow.
The Problem

We've Romanticized Spontaneity.

The story we tell ourselves: "We let the Spirit lead. We stay flexible. We don't want a plan so locked we miss the moment."

That sounds humble. It's lazy.

The Spirit can move through a plan. Preparation creates margin for the Spirit to move — because you're not standing in the hallway figuring out who's in kids right now.

Preparation is not the opposite of Spirit-led. Preparation is what Spirit-led people do before the moment arrives.
Luke 14:28
"Suppose one of you wants to build a tower. Won't you first sit down and estimate the cost?"

Jesus said count the cost before you build. That's pre-production. That's a shot list.

You don't get to build what you haven't surveyed. You don't get to tell what you haven't planned.
What a Shot List Is

Four Questions.
Answered Before Sunday.

A shot list is a pre-production document that answers four questions before any Sunday or event begins.

01
What moments do we need to capture?
02
What story are we trying to tell?
03
Who is responsible for each piece of coverage?
04
What will we do with it after we have it?

If the team you lead can't answer all four before Sunday — they are guessing.

Guessing is expensive. It costs you shots you can never get back. Moments that happened once and will never happen again.
One person trying to capture everything captures nothing. Intentional coverage by a focused team captures everything that matters.
Five Coverage Categories

Every Sunday Needs These.

Not whoever has time. A specific volunteer. A specific assignment. A specific time window.

01 · The Atmosphere
The room before people arrive. The lighting at its peak. The stage design. The details that tell the story of how much you care before a single word is spoken. Fifteen-minute window. Own it or lose it.
The room tells the first story. Most churches never photograph it.
02 · The People
The greeting at the door. The parent handing off a child in kids. The first-time guest receiving their welcome gift. Unstaged. Predictable. Happening every Sunday — because this church has rhythms.
The moments that feel spontaneous to the congregation are predictable to the team. Position for them.
03 · The Worship Experience
Not background footage. Content. Wide establishing shots. Congregation sections. Close-ups of individual worshipers — faces, hands, posture. Band. Singers. Worship leader. If you only have one shooter, they need to know which shot matters more. Not a guess. A plan.
Without a plan, you'll get the same shot twenty-five times and call it coverage. It's not. It's one shot, twenty-five times.
04 · The Teaching Moment
Not just the pastor on camera. The congregation's response. The moment the message lands. The person three rows back whose hand goes to their chest when a sentence hits them.
The preacher is the message. The congregation is the evidence. Both need to be on your shot list.
05 · The Response
The altar call. The baptism. The prayer moment. The entire point of the Sunday. The category most teams miss — because they're not ready. Know where to stand before it happens.
The response is the proof of what God did. If you don't capture it, you can't testify to it.
Building the Document

Four Parts.
Write It Down.

The shot list covers four things. Miss one — you're still guessing.

01 · The Date & The Event
Name the Sunday.
What Sunday is this? What series? Any special moments — baptisms, guest speakers — requiring specific attention?
02 · The Assignments
Every volunteer knows their lane.
Every volunteer should read the list and know exactly where they are and what they're responsible for. If the names aren't on paper, the responsibility isn't real.
03 · The Timeline
When each shot gets made.
Atmosphere shots happen before doors open. Response coverage doesn't start at the altar call — it starts when they position themselves before it happens.
04 · The Deliverables
Know where it's going before you shoot it.
Reels need vertical. Print needs wide. Website needs variety. Say it before Sunday. The destination changes what you prioritize.
If the team doesn't know what the content is for, they can't shoot it right. Give them the destination before the journey.
The Briefing

Ten Minutes.
Non-Negotiable.

Before every significant Sunday, the team needs a briefing. Not a text. Not a group message. A meeting — even if it's ten minutes.

Everyone reviews the shot list. Everyone asks questions. Everyone knows who owns what. Everyone leaves with a clear assignment and a clear picture of success.

A team that's briefed makes field decisions that align with the plan. A team that isn't briefed makes decisions that serve themselves.

As a volunteer lead, this is the single most important ten minutes you run all week. It's also what proves — to the staff above you and the volunteers beside you — that you take this seriously.

You brief before you deploy. Every time. Without exception.
The volunteer who briefs their team is already operating at staff level. Budget catches up to standard. It always does.
Planning creates margin. Margin creates flexibility. Flexibility is what lets you catch the moment nobody planned for.
The Call

Build Your Shot List.
Before You Leave.

Pull out your phone. Answer these four questions for next Sunday before you walk out of this room.

01
What five moments absolutely cannot be missed next Sunday — the ones that, if we don't capture them, we failed? Write them down.
02
Who on the team owns each one? Agree with them now. Not this week. Now. Volunteers don't get assigned — they commit. Get the commitment.
03
What are we doing with the content after? Name a specific platform and a specific timeline.
04
When are we briefing the team? Put it on the calendar right now. Protect it like a call time.

Do those four before you walk out — you'll have more direction than most creative teams walk into Sunday with. Paid or volunteer.

Lock It In
What is your single most important shot next Sunday?
One moment. One line. We'll check in before Sunday.

The Close

Testimony. Not Content.

Every moment this church experiences — the baptism, the first-time guest, the kid who raised their hand, the marriage saved in altar prayer — every one deserves to be told.

Not because they're content. Because they're testimony.

The team you lead is the keeper of those testimonies. You carry it better when you're prepared.

Habakkuk 2:2
"Write down the revelation and make it plain on tablets so that a herald may run with it."
Your camera is not a tool for social media. It is an instrument for testimony. Use it with that weight.
If you don't have a shot list, you're guessing. And what God is doing in this church is too important to guess at.
Write it down. Make it plain. Then go get the shot.
