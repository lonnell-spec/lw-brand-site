V · THE PROMPT IS THE WORK
Same AI · Same 30 seconds · Three different prompts

Build me a meeting agenda.

TIER 1 · LAZY
"Make me a meeting agenda."
Generic 6-item template. Welcome → Updates → Discussion → Action Items → Next Steps → Close. Useless.
TIER 2 · DETAILED
"Build a 45-minute agenda for our weekly team meeting. 8 people. Cover updates, decisions, action items."
Structured, time-blocked, decent — but still templated. You'll need to edit.
TIER 3 · SPECIFIC
"Build a 45-min agenda for our staff meeting Tuesday. 8 people. Start with one win from each person. Cover: Sunday recap, Multiply progress, two decisions (event budget + volunteer schedule). Every action item needs an owner and a date. Close with prayer. Warm voice. Print-ready."
Production-ready. Walks out the door usable.

The AI didn't get smarter. The human got clearer.

VI · WHAT A SKILL ACTUALLY LOOKS LIKE
It's not magic · it's plain text

Skills are just markdown files.

A "skill" is a plain text file with simple formatting — headings, lists, bold text. It tells Claude how to do one specific thing: write a newsletter in your voice, run a meeting recap, format a brand-aligned email.

If you can read English, you can read a skill. If you can read it, you can change it. If you can change it, you can write one.

SKILL.md
--- name: newsletter-weekly description: Write the weekly newsletter in our church's voice — warm, clear, bold. --- # When to use Use this whenever drafting the weekly church email. # Voice rules - Warm, direct, no jargon. - Lead with a story, not an announcement. - Cite scripture sparingly. Always relevant. # Structure 1. Hook (1 sentence) 2. Story or insight (3-4 lines) 3. Three updates with names + actions 4. Sunday CTA

XII · WHAT TO WATCH FOR
The other side of the coin

AI lies
confidently.

01

Double-check the work.

It sounds just as sure when it's wrong — names, dates, scripture, numbers. It is not always right. You verify before it goes anywhere.

02

Don't let it run on its own.

It's a partner, not autopilot. Stay in the loop — you read it, you approve it, you send it.

03

Make it challenge itself.

Don't take the first answer. "Push back as a contrarian." "Now answer as a business owner." "Now as a ministry worker." Different seats — better thinking.

XII · WHAT TO WATCH FOR
The shepherd's rule

Some things never go in.

01

Names attached to stories.

Counseling conversations. Prayer requests with identities. Pastoral care details. If you must ask about a situation, strip the name first — "a member is walking through X," never who.

02

Other people's data.

Giving records. Contact lists. Anything you'd need permission to forward, you need permission to paste.

03

The rule of thumb.

If you wouldn't email it to a stranger, don't paste it into a personal AI account. Team and enterprise accounts carry real protections — ask what your church has before church data goes anywhere.

People trust you with their lives. That trust is the one thing AI can't give back if you spend it.
